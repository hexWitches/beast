Ontology Specification Draft

Abstract

The BEAST ontology provides a formal representation of creatures, whether inspired by real or imaginary, focusing on their context origin, their textual attestations and their physical and symbolic evolution across different periods of time.

Introduction back to ToC

The BEAST Ontology is a formal framework designed to model, track, and analyze real and imaginary creatures across historical texts and modern media. It is structured in three interconnected modules: Provenance, Biological and Symbolic. Linking the biological aspects with the deep cultural/symbolic meaning, allowing us to understand the changes in the general collective mentality. The ontology maps a symbolic evolution of the beasts with a cross-domain reasoning, in compliance with the latest standards of the Semanti Web by utilizing OWL (Web Ontology Language).

BEAST Ontology: Description back to ToC

The BEAST ontology is structured into three interconnected modules. The Provenance Module documents the historical and textual footprint of creatures, distinguishing between the universal concept of a beast (Creature) and its source-specific manifestations (Interpretation) across bestiaries, literary works, visual artworks, and cinematic works; it also tracks the diachronic evolution of creature terminology through Nomenclature and Etymology. The Biological Module defines the morphological and behavioural traits of both real and imaginary beings: it catalogs anatomical composition, habitats, diets, and abilities, and documents anomalies in real animals through a structured vocabulary of absence, duplication, and modification. The Symbolic Module provides a multidimensional framework for the analysis of creatures as cultural and archetypal figures. It integrates two analytical layers: Gilbert Durand's anthropological structures of the imaginary (DurandImaginary scheme) and an archetype and narrative function layer that distinguishes the universal psychological patterns creatures embody from the contextual roles they perform in specific sources. Together the three modules enable the ontology to trace a creature from its historical attestation in a primary text, through its biological description, to its deepest symbolic resonance in the collective imagination.

Biological Moduleni back to ToC or Named Individual ToC

IRI: https://w3id.org/beast/ontology#BiologicalModule

The Biological Module catalogs the physical and behavioural traits of creatures — whether real or imaginary — as described in Bestiaries and other Sources. It distinguishes real animals from imaginary beings, and further classifies the latter into composite and hybrid creatures whose anatomy is derived from two or more biological sources. For real animals it documents deformities and monstrous irregularities through a structured vocabulary of anatomical anomaly: absence of a canonical part, duplication, and modification. For imaginary beings it records the anatomical parts they possess and the real animals, plants, or inanimate elements from which those parts are derived. Across all creatures the module captures habitats — distinguishing real geographic environments from mythological or imaginary spaces — diets, and abilities. This layer serves as the primary morphological record that the Symbolic Module then interprets as archetypal and cultural meaning.

Cyclical symbol represents the archetype of the Eternal Return and time as a series of phases. Using the moon as a primary model - being the first to die and the first to be reborn - these symbols suggest that time is a series of phases where opposites (life/death, light/dark) coexist. This 'lunar optimism' does not negate death but reintegrates it as an essential stage of an incessant, evolving movement where every sunset is a prelude to a new dawn. The repetition of opposites is also linked to evolution: it is not merely a continuous repetition of the same events. Things that change may also evolve or move forward; they may progress.

A conceptual scheme providing a framework for the symbolic and anthropological analysis of creatures within bestiaries. It acts as the theoretical pivot of the Symbolic Module, allowing for the classification of beasts and their attributes based on their relationship with human anxiety regarding time and death. Provenance Module (the Anchor)ni back to ToC or Named Individual ToC

IRI: https://w3id.org/beast/ontology#ProvenanceModule

The Provenance Module documents the historical and textual footprint of every creature in the ontology. It distinguishes between the universal, abstract concept of a beast (Creature) and its source-specific manifestations (Interpretation), allowing the ontology to track how a single entity is described, depicted, or reinterpreted across different authors, periods, and media — from medieval manuscripts to contemporary film. It anchors each Interpretation to the primary Source in which it appears and classifies Sources by their genre and intertextual relationships through :cites and :wasInfluencedBy.

Symbolic Moduleni back to ToC or Named Individual ToC

IRI: https://w3id.org/beast/ontology#SymbolicModule

The Symbolic Module represents the analytical and interpretive layer of the ontology, where art history, literary theory, depth psychology, and anthropology converge. It provides two interlocking frameworks for the symbolic analysis of creatures and their source-specific Interpretations. First, the DurandImaginary scheme applies Gilbert Durand's anthropological structures of the imaginary to classify creatures according to the Regime (Diurnal or Nocturnal) and structural logic (Schizomorphic, Mystical, or Synthetic) that governs their symbolic imagery. Second, the archetype and narrative function layer — through :Archetype, :NarrativeFunction, :embodiesArchetype, and :hasNarrativeFunction — distinguishes between the universal psychological pattern a creature instantiates across all its manifestations and the specific contextual role it performs within a given Source. The module also captures symbolic divergences at the level of Interpretation: the properties :additionalPart, :removedPart, :additionalAbility, :removedAbility, :alternativeDiet and :alternativeHabitat document how a specific author or tradition departs from the canonical image of a creature, and what symbolic meaning those departures carry.
